Language models learn statistical structure from sequences of tokens.
This small corpus exists only to validate the local data and tokenizer pipeline.

Reproducible experiments require stable inputs, explicit configuration, and
artifacts that can be inspected after a run.

Streaming processing lets the platform handle data larger than available RAM.
Checksums make it possible to detect changed or corrupted source files.

Training from scratch starts with random weights and improves them by predicting
the next token in many examples. A validation split measures generalization on
documents that were not used for parameter updates during the training run.

The smoke pipeline deliberately uses a tiny model and a tiny local corpus. Its
purpose is to exercise artifact creation, deterministic configuration, checkpoint
resume, validation metrics, and generation before expensive data preparation.
